Utica Greens

Utica greens was made popular by the owner of Chesterfield’s restaurant in Utica NY. Joe Morelle was the owner and chef. He observed a chef who worked at Grimaldi’s restaurant, one time in 1988, when Morelle was an employee there. He adjusted the recipe and a hit was made. When Chesterfield’s restaurant opened, it was served as an appetizer on the menu. This is an old-time dish made by ancestors from Italy. It is seen at home tables of just greens or called “scrole”, garlic and olive oil. Then things were added to make it a better dish by adding things like hot peppers, breadcrumbs, proscitto, potatoes and Parmigiano-Reggiano cheese making it an amazing dish. There are many variations of the famous dish, but Morelle’s version is the most well-known. The recipe for Utica Greens or Greens Morelle: INGREDIENTS
FOR THE OREGANATO:
½ cup extra-virgin olive oil
1 cup bread crumbs
½ cup Parmigiano-Reggiano, grated

FOR THE GREENS:
1 head escarole, about 1 1/4 pound, bottom removed,
leaves separated and washed thoroughly to remove grit
2 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil
4 slices prosciutto (about 2 ounces), sliced thin and cut into roughly 1-inch squares
4 to 6 hot cherry peppers (pickled will do if you can't find fresh), tops and seeds removed, broken by hand or chopped into 4 or 5 chunks
2 cloves garlic, minced
½ teaspoon kosher salt
¼ teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
½ cup Parmigiano-Reggiano, grated, plus 2 or more tablespoons for garnish
PREPARATION
Bring a large pot of water to boil over high heat. Prepare an ice bath in a large bowl and set aside. Meanwhile, in a medium bowl, prepare the oreganato: Mix the oil, bread crumbs and cheese until well blended. It should have the texture of moist beach sand. Set aside. When the water is boiling, blanch the greens until they are nearly limp but still a little firm, about 1 to 2 minutes. Plunge them into the ice bath to stop the cooking. Remove and drain them well in a colander or salad spinner, allowing them to remain moist but not dripping wet. Squeeze just a little of the moisture from them, then chop them into 2-inch pieces. Set aside. Heat the broiler. Coat a large pan with the olive oil. Over medium heat, sauté the prosciutto, cherry peppers and garlic until the prosciutto is browned and slightly crisp, about 5 minutes. (Lower heat slightly if garlic begins to brown too quickly.) Add the chopped greens to the pan, season with salt and pepper and stir to mix well. Add 1/2 cup Parmigiano-Reggiano and 1/2 cup of the oreganato. Cook 3 to 4 minutes, stirring frequently to blend, scraping the bottom of the pan to keep the oreganato from burning. Sprinkle another 1/4 cup oreganato atop the greens. (Save leftover oreganato for use in another greens dish, or add it to baked chicken or shrimp.) Place the pan under the broiler and broil until the top browns, about 2 minutes. Remove from the broiler and sprinkle a bit more cheese on the dish. Serve immediately.

Halfmoons

Hemstroughts, Gingerbread bake shop, Holland farms The story of the Half Moon cookie Round chocolate cookie that is frosted half chocolate and half vanilla. Originated in the early 20th century by Hemstroughts bakery. Where there are some versions in NYC but it is a glazed hard cookie not a frosted soft cookie. The original Half moon cookie had a chocolate drop cookie bottom with half chocolate frosting and half vanilla frosting. Now, different bakeries in the Utica area have different varieties of the cookie from vanilla bottom, carrot cake bottom , peanut butter frosted, red velvet bottom. The possibilities are endless. The best places to buy these are Hemstroughts bakery your local grocery stores, Holland Farms in Yorkville Gingerbread Bake shop in Washington Mills( my favorite) The owner at of the Gingerbread bake shop once worked for Hemstroughts and when Hemstroughts went out of business Jerry Hoban opened the Gingerbread bake shop and took the recipe with him and changed it a bit to make it his own.. Chicken Riggies

Well there are many stories of how Chicken Riggies was invented. But they all eem to start with the Clinton House in Clinton NY. The story says that in 1979, on Monday nights, the lawyers, doctors and union workers would come in to play cards and the chefs would make this dish for them. So over the years many restaurants would make their own variation of it but the most notable one is Chesterfield’s Restaurant in Utica. They have been making it since 1989.
