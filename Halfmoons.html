Halfmoons

Round chocolate cookie that is frosted half chocolate and half vanilla. Originated in the early 20th century by Hemstroughts bakery. Where there are some versions in NYC but it is a glazed hard cookie not a frosted soft cookie. The original Half moon cookie had a chocolate drop cookie bottom with half chocolate frosting and half vanilla frosting. Now, different bakeries in the Utica area have different varieties of the cookie from vanilla bottom, carrot cake bottom , peanut butter frosted, red velvet bottom. The possibilities are endless. The best places to buy these are Hemstroughts bakery in your local grocery stores, Holland Farms in Yorkville Gingerbread Bake shop in Washington Mills( my favorite) The owner at of the Gingerbread bake shop once worked for Hemstroughts and when Hemstroughts went out of business Jerry Hoban opened the Gingerbread bake shop and took the recipe with him and changed it a bit to make it his own.. Below is the famous recipe for the Hemstroughts Bakery cookie.

Recipe
Ingredients COOKIES
3 3/4 c all purpose flour
3/4 tspbaking powder
2 tsp baking soda
2 1/4 c granulated sugar
1 c butter, cubed
3/4 c cocoa powder, sifted
1/4 tsp salt
2 large eggs
1 tsp pure vanilla extract
1 1/2 c milk

FROSTING - CHOCOLATE

3 1/2 oz bittersweet chocolate
3 1/2 oz semi-sweet chocolate
1 Tbsp butter
4 1/3 c confectioner's sugar
2 Tbsp corn syrup
1 tsp pure vanilla extract
1 pinch salt
1 c boiling water

FROSTING - VANILLA

7 c confectioners' sugar
1 c butter, cubed, room temp
1/2 c vegetable shortening
7 Tbsp milk
1 Tbsp pure vanilla extract
1 pinch Salt

Preheat oven to 350°. Sift together flour, baking powder, and baking soda in a medium bowl and set aside. Put sugar, butter, cocoa and salt in bowl of mixer and beat on medium speed until fluffy. Add eggs and vanilla and continue to beat. Add half the milk, then half the flour mixture, beating after each addition until smooth; repeat with remaining milk and flour mixture. Spoon or pipe batter onto parchment-lined baking sheets, making 3'' rounds 2'' apart. Bake until cookies are set, about 12 minutes. Allow to cool, then remove from parchment. Frosting - Chocolate: Melt chocolates and butter in the top of a double boiler over simmering water over medium heat. Add confectioners’ sugar, corn syrup, vanilla, salt and 6 tbsp. boiling water - mix to a smooth, stiff paste with a rubber spatula. Thin icing with up to 8 tbsp. more boiling water. Icing should fall from a spoon in thick ribbons. Keep warm in a double boiler over low heat. Frosting - Vanilla: Put sugar, butter, shortening, milk, vanilla and salt in a bowl. Beat on low speed to mix, then increase to medium and beat until light and fluffy. Using a metal spatula, spread about 1 tbsp. of warm fudge icing on half of the flat side of each cookie. Spread the other half of each cookie with 1 heaping tbsp. vanilla icing.

Chicken Riggies

History of Chicken Riggies Well there are many stories of how Chicken Riggies was invented. But they all eem to start with the Clinton House in Clinton NY. The story says that in 1979, on Monday nights, the lawyers, doctors and union workers would come in to play cards and the chefs would make this dish for them. So over the years many restaurants would make their own variation of it but the most notable one is Chesterfield’s Restaurant in Utica. They have been making it since 1989.

Utica Greens

Utica greens was made popular by the owner of Chesterfield’s restaurant in Utica NY. Joe Morelle was the owner and chef. He observed a chef who worked at Grimaldi’s restaurant, one time in 1988, when Morelle was a n employee there. He adjusted the recipe and a hit was made. When Chesterfield’s restaurant opened, it was served as an appetizer on the menu. This is an old-time dish made by ancestors from Italy. It is seen at home tables of just greens or called “scrole”, garlic and olive oil. Then things were added to make it a better dish by adding things like hot peppers, breadcrumbs, proscitto, potatoes and Parmigiano-Reggiano cheese making it an amazing dish. There are many variations of the famous dish, but Morelle’s version is the most well-known.

Tomato Pie

In the early 1900’s the Italian-American pie make it debut. No one really knows where it started but O’Scugnizzo’s Pizza on Bleeker St started to sell it in 1914, and Napolis bakery in Utica has been making it for more than 50 years. Its thick light focaccia like dough that is lathered in tomato sauce and adding Pecorino Romano cheese and oregano makes this pie incredible. You don’t want to serve this hot or refrigerated. Room temperature st. You also never have to worry about it going bad because it is gone before it can. It is amazing dish that I have loved since I was a kid.
